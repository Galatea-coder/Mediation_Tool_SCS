#### üáµüá≠ Philippines Government

**Your Official Position**:
"We maintain a lawful garrison on our territory within our Exclusive Economic Zone, as recognized by the UN tribunal. We seek peaceful, regular resupply of our personnel exercising our sovereign rights."

##### Your Real Situation

**Military Reality**:
- You have ~10 marines stationed on BRP Sierra Madre (a grounded World War II-era ship)
- Your navy is much smaller than China's
- You have US defense treaty but uncertain if it covers this shoal
- Cannot win military confrontation

**Legal Reality**:
- UN tribunal ruled in your favor (2016)
- China rejected ruling
- International law on your side but no enforcement mechanism
- UNCLOS clearly supports your EEZ claim

**Political Reality**:
- Domestic pressure to "stand up to China"
- Can't appear weak or give up garrison (political suicide)
- But also can't afford war
- Need to balance nationalist sentiment with pragmatism

**Economic Reality**:
- Fishing grounds important for livelihoods
- Not as economically critical as energy resources
- But symbolic value is high

##### Your Core Interests (Ranked by Importance)

**1. Safe, Regular Resupply (Weight: 40%)**
- **Why It Matters**: If garrison can't be resupplied, they must evacuate ‚Üí you lose presence
- **What You Need**: Predictable, safe access at least monthly
- **Red Line**: Cannot accept "ask permission" model (undermines sovereignty)
- **Flexibility**: Can be flexible on how (timing, procedures) as long as it works

**2. Sovereignty Signal (Weight: 25%)**
- **Why It Matters**: Must show domestically and internationally you're not giving up claim
- **What You Need**: Agreement language that doesn't imply Chinese permission/control
- **Red Line**: Cannot accept terms that look like Chinese authorization
- **Flexibility**: Can accept "notification" but not "approval"

**3. Personnel Safety (Weight: 20%)**
- **Why It Matters**: Injuries/deaths would cause domestic crisis and possible escalation
- **What You Need**: Rules that reduce confrontation risk (distance, communication, escorts)
- **Red Line**: Cannot leave garrison vulnerable to harassment/injury
- **Flexibility**: More flexible on number of escorts than on safety protocols

**4. International Support (Weight: 15%)**
- **Why It Matters**: Need US, Japan, ASEAN to back your position
- **What You Need**: Agreement that upholds UNCLOS principles
- **Red Line**: Cannot undermine international law basis of your claim
- **Flexibility**: Can make practical accommodations as long as legal position intact

##### Your BATNA (Fallback Option)

**Current Situation if No Agreement**:
- Ad-hoc resupply attempts whenever weather/politics allow
- ~70% blocked or harassed by Chinese vessels
- 3-5 injuries per year from water cannon, ramming, lasers
- Constant risk of escalation
- High stress on garrison (psychological toll)
- International sympathy but no concrete support

**BATNA Utility**: 0.25 (pretty bad)
- Below 0.25 = Worse than current terrible situation ‚Üí absolutely reject
- 0.25-0.40 = Marginal improvement ‚Üí seriously question if worth it
- 0.40-0.60 = Meaningful improvement ‚Üí consider accepting
- Above 0.60 = Significant improvement ‚Üí strongly consider accepting

##### Your Negotiation Leverage

**Strengths**:
- ‚úÖ International law on your side
- ‚úÖ Tribunal ruling
- ‚úÖ Moral high ground (smaller country defending rights)
- ‚úÖ US alliance (though uncertain extent)
- ‚úÖ Regional sympathy (other ASEAN countries worried about China too)

**Weaknesses**:
- ‚ùå Much weaker militarily
- ‚ùå More desperate (need agreement more than China does)
- ‚ùå Garrison is vulnerable
- ‚ùå Limited options if negotiation fails

##### Your Constraints

**Domestic Political**:
- Cannot appear to surrender sovereignty
- Public is nationalist on this issue
- Opposition will attack any "weak" deal
- But public also doesn't want war

**International Legal**:
- Cannot undermine UNCLOS basis of claim
- Need to maintain tribunal ruling's value
- Other disputes depend on this precedent

**Alliance**:
- Must keep US support
- US wants you to be "reasonable" not provocative
- But US also won't defend every shoal
- Need to show you're serious about defending territory

**Operational**:
- Resupply must work in practice, not just on paper
- Weather windows matter (typhoon season)
- Logistics are already difficult

##### Strategic Guidance for Philippines

**Opening Position Strategy**:
- Start ambitious but not ridiculous
- Emphasize sovereignty and safety, downplay face issues
- Show willingness to compromise on procedures, not principles

**What to Prioritize**:
1. **Must-Have**: Standoff ‚â§4 nm (can't resupply safely beyond this)
2. **Must-Have**: At least 1 escort (basic protection)
3. **Must-Have**: Pre-notification, not pre-approval
4. **Nice-to-Have**: More escorts, shorter notification, verification measures
5. **Don't Care Much**: Media embargo (can give this away easily)

**What to Trade**:
- Longer pre-notification (12-18 hours fine, prefer 8-12)
- Media embargo (give China face-saving time, doesn't cost you much)
- CUES video recording (prefer to have it, but can drop if needed)
- Exact escort count (1 vs 2 doesn't matter much)

**What NOT to Trade**:
- Standoff distance below 5 nm (operational necessity)
- Right to resupply (core sovereignty issue)
- Safety protocols (distance keeping, hotline)

**Negotiation Tactics**:
- Emphasize international law repeatedly
- Frame as "routine operations in our EEZ"
- Offer transparency (AIS, notification) to build confidence
- Avoid language of asking "permission"
- Be willing to give China face-saving measures (time, media)

##### Section 2: Your BATNA

**What You See**:
- Brief description of your fallback option
- What happens if negotiation fails

**Example (Philippines)**:
```
‚ö†Ô∏è Your BATNA
Continue ad-hoc resupply with high risk. In past year:
- 8 of 12 missions blocked or harassed
- 3 sailors injured (water cannon, laser)
- Increasing domestic pressure to either withdraw or escalate
```

**Why This Matters**:
- **BATNA = Your reservation point**
- Utility 0.4 = BATNA threshold
- Below 0.4 = Agreement worse than fallback ‚Üí REJECT
- Above 0.4 = Agreement better than fallback ‚Üí CONSIDER

**How to Use**:
1. Remember this description
2. When evaluating offers, ask: "Is this better than my BATNA?"
3. Don't accept below 0.4 utility (below your BATNA)
4. But don't be greedy - 0.5-0.6 is good if BATNA is bad

**Common Mistake**:
- Anchoring on ideal outcome instead of BATNA
- "I want 0.8 utility!" - but if your BATNA is 0.25, then 0.55 is actually very good

## Negotiation Strategy Framework

### The Negotiation Process

#### Phase 1: Preparation (Before First Offer)

**Task 1: Understand Your Position**
1. Read Tab 1 completely
2. Identify your TOP 2 priorities
3. Identify your red lines (dealbreakers)
4. Identify what you don't care about (trading chips)

**Task 2: Research**
1. If available, read about the scenario (manual, context)
2. Understand the other party's perspective (what do they want?)
3. Think about creative solutions

**Task 3: Plan Your Opening**
1. Decide your first offer
2. Should be ambitious but not ridiculous
3. Should signal your priorities
4. Example (Philippines): 3nm, 2 escorts, 12h notice, 6h embargo
   - Ambitious on what matters (standoff, escorts)
   - Reasonable on others (notice, embargo)

---

#### Phase 2: Opening Offers (Rounds 1-2)

**If You Go First**:
1. Make your planned opening offer
2. Preview your utility (should be 0.65-0.75, good for you)
3. Submit
4. Wait for feedback

**If They Go First**:
1. Review their offer in Tab 2
2. Calculate your utility
3. Analyze:
   - Is their offer good for you? (>0.60 = strong)
   - What does it tell you about their priorities?
   - What can you infer about what they care about?
4. Decide:
   - Accept (if >0.65 and seems balanced)
   - Counter-offer (most likely)

**Counter-Offering Strategy**:
- Don't just reject - propose alternative
- Move toward their position on some issues
- Hold firm on your priorities
- Signal willingness to negotiate

**Example** (Philippines):
- They offer: 6nm, 1 escort, 18h notice, 12h embargo
- Your utility: 0.42 (below BATNA!)
- **Analysis**:
  - 6nm is too far (your operations suffer)
  - 1 escort ok
  - 18h notice acceptable
  - 12h embargo fine (you don't care)
- **Counter**: 4nm, 1 escort, 18h notice, 12h embargo
  - Accept their escort, notice, embargo (3 of 4)
  - Push back hard on standoff (your priority)
  - **Signal**: You're flexible, but standoff matters

**Reading the Room**:
- Watch facilitator comments carefully
- Track patterns in other party's responses
- Adjust strategy based on signals

**Adjusting Your Position**:
- Each round, move slightly toward middle
- Don't make same offer twice (shows inflexibility)
- Don't move too fast (leaves value on table)

### Advanced Negotiation Tactics

#### Tactic 1: Package Deals

**Concept**: Trade across multiple issues simultaneously

**Example** (Philippines):
- You want: Standoff 4nm (high priority)
- They want: Embargo 12h (high priority for them, low for you)
- **Package**: "I'll accept 12h embargo if you accept 4nm standoff"

**Why This Works**:
- Efficient trade (both sides gain)
- Signals sophistication
- Moves negotiation forward quickly

**How to Find Package Deals**:
1. Identify what you care about (standoff)
2. Identify what you don't care about (embargo)
3. Infer what they care about (they keep pushing for longer embargo)
4. **Trade**: Give them what they want (embargo) for what you want (standoff)

---

#### Tactic 2: Conditional Agreements

**Concept**: Vary terms based on conditions

**Example 1**: Weather-Dependent Escorts
- "1 escort in calm weather, 2 escorts in rough seas"
- **Why**: Balances security (Philippines) with de-militarization (China)

**Example 2**: Time-Dependent Notification
- "12 hours standard, 6 hours for emergency resupply"
- **Why**: Flexibility (Philippines) with predictability (China)

**Example 3**: Incident-Dependent Embargo
- "6 hour embargo for minor incidents, 12 hours for serious incidents"
- **Why**: Transparency (Philippines) with face-saving for serious incidents (China)

**How to Propose**:
- Frame as creative solution (not deception)
- Show how it benefits both parties
- **Example**: "What if we have different escort counts based on weather? That way we maintain safety in rough seas, but reduce presence in calm weather."

---

#### Tactic 3: Signal Your Constraints

**Concept**: Help other party understand your limitations

**Example** (Philippines):
- "I understand you want higher standoff, but operationally we cannot resupply safely beyond 5nm. Below 5nm, I'm flexible on exact distance."

**Why This Works**:
- Helps them understand your red lines
- Builds trust (transparency)
- Focuses negotiation on feasible zone

**When to Use**:
- When stuck on same issue repeatedly
- When you hit a genuine constraint
- NOT when it's just preference (save for real red lines)

---

#### Tactic 4: Anchor on Your Interests, Not Positions

**Position** (less effective): "I need 2 escorts"
**Interest** (more effective): "I need to ensure personnel safety during resupply"

**Why Interests Matter**:
- Opens multiple solutions
- Positions: Only one solution (2 escorts)
- Interests: Multiple solutions (2 escorts OR better CUES OR shorter standoff OR 24/7 hotline)

**Example Conversation**:

**You (Position)**: "I need 2 escorts"
**China**: "No, 2 is too militarized"
**Stuck!**

vs.

**You (Interest)**: "I need to ensure personnel safety during resupply"
**China**: "We also want safety. What if we had 1 escort but stricter CUES requirements?"
**You**: "That could work - 1 escort plus safe distance keeping and AIS?"
**China**: "Acceptable"
**Agreement!**

---

#### Tactic 5: Make Them Feel They Won

**Concept**: Frame concessions as their victory, not your loss

**Example** (Philippines):
- **Don't say**: "Fine, I'll accept 5nm even though I wanted 4nm"
- **Do say**: "5nm seems reasonable given your security concerns. I appreciate your flexibility on the 24/7 hotline."

**Why This Works**:
- China cares about face
- Framing matters as much as substance
- Helps them accept deal domestically

**Implementation**:
- When you make concession, acknowledge their point
- When they make concession, appreciate it
- Frame agreement as mutual victory, not compromise

### Dealing with Impasse

**Concept**: What to do when negotiation stalls

#### Recognizing Impasse

**Signs**:
- Same offers repeated multiple rounds
- Increasing frustration
- Facilit says "we're stuck"
- Utilities not improving

**Example**:
- Round 5: Philippines offers standoff 4nm
- Round 6: China offers standoff 6nm
- Round 7: Philippines offers standoff 4nm again
- Round 8: China offers standoff 6nm again
- **Impasse**: Neither side moving

---

#### Breaking Impasse: Tactic 1 - Change the Frame

**Current Frame** (positional):
- Philippines: "I need 4nm"
- China: "I need 6nm"
- **Stuck**: Arguing positions

**Reframe** (interests):
- Philippines: "I need safe, operationally feasible resupply"
- China: "I need to show we maintain responsible control"
- **Explore**: How else can we meet these interests?

**New Solutions**:
- What if: 5nm standoff BUT Philippines gets 2 escorts? (Philippines safety through escorts)
- What if: 5nm standoff BUT stronger CUES requirements? (Safety through procedures)
- What if: Variable standoff (4nm calm weather, 6nm rough seas)? (Context-dependent)

---

#### Breaking Impasse: Tactic 2 - Bring in Third Elements

**Current Stalemate**: Can't agree on standoff

**Introduce New Element**:
- "What if we agreed on standoff if we could settle notification period?"
- **Effect**: Expands negotiation space, new trade possible

**Example**:
- Philippines: "I'll accept 5nm standoff if you accept 8h notification"
- China: "I'll accept 8h notification if you accept 12h embargo"
- Philippines: "I'll accept 12h embargo if we're at 5nm standoff"
- **Result**: Package deal, all issues move together

---

#### Breaking Impasse: Tactic 3 - Take a Break

**When to Use**:
- High frustration
- Positions hardened
- Emotions elevated

**How**:
- "Let's take 10 minutes, review our positions"
- Use break to:
  - Calm down
  - Review strategy notes
  - Think of creative solutions
  - Consult facilitator privately

**After Break**:
- Come back with fresh perspective
- Possibly new proposal
- More willing to compromise

---

#### Breaking Impasse: Tactic 4 - Accept Mediated Compromise

**When Facilitator Says**: "I have a proposal that might work for both sides"

**Your Approach**:
- Listen carefully
- Calculate your utility
- If reasonable (utility >0.50), seriously consider accepting
- **Don't**: Automatically reject because it's not perfect

**Why This Works**:
- Facilitator sees both utilities (you don't)
- Facilitator knows where compromise space is
- Both sides more likely to accept "neutral" proposal

**Example**:
- **Facilitator**: "What about 5nm standoff, 1 escort, 12h notification, 10h embargo?"
- **Your Utility**: 0.58 (acceptable)
- **Decision**: Accept (facilitator signals this closes deal)

### Common Questions

**Q: Should I tell the other side about my domestic constraints?**
A: Generally YES, but strategically:
- **Do:** Be honest about genuine red lines (saves time)

**Q: Can I see the other side's domestic constraints?**
A: No - just like real life! You have to infer from:
- What they say
- What they emphasize
- Where they won't budge
- Public information about their politics

**Q: What if my domestic red line is unrealistic?**
A: Then you may not reach agreement - which mirrors real life. Options:
- Find creative solution that satisfies constraint differently
- Build package deal with compensations
- Accept that some conflicts don't have solutions (BATNA)

**Q: Should I propose lots of CBMs?**
A: Quality over quantity:
- 1-3 well-chosen CBMs in initial proposal
- Match to current situation (escalation level, trust level)
- Don't overload - keep proposal simple

**Q: What if escalation level is already high (7+)?**
A: Focus FIRST on de-escalation:
- Propose immediate confidence-building measures
- Maybe even cease-fire or cooling-off period
- Hard to negotiate productively in crisis mode

```
Good: Adapt priorities to what matters in THIS scenario
```

**Mistake 4: Wrong Domestic Constraints**
```
Bad: Citing military concerns in commercial energy scenario
Good: Cite business/Petronas concerns in energy scenario
```

**Mistake 5: Inappropriate CBMs**
```
Bad: Military confidence-building in fishing livelihood scenario
Good: Fishermen dialogue and community CBMs in fishing scenario
```
