## Overview

The Strategic Levers System enhances the SCS Mediator simulation by adding **soft power dimensions** to complement numerical military parameters. This creates a more realistic model of diplomatic negotiations where success depends not just on military positioning, but also on:

- Diplomatic capital and influence
- International legitimacy and support
- Domestic political backing
- Credibility and reputation

### Key Benefits

**For Facilitators**:
- Richer debrief discussions about why teams succeed or fail
- Quantifiable soft power metrics to analyze
- More realistic escalation risk assessments
- Clear visibility into each party's strategic positioning

**For Participants**:
- Strategic actions that complement parameter adjustments
- Clear feedback on soft power positioning
- AI guidance that recommends both parameters AND strategic moves
- Realistic modeling of diplomatic constraints

## What Are Strategic Levers?

Strategic Levers are **high-level diplomatic actions** that affect both:
1. **Hard parameters** (military positioning, patrol patterns, etc.)
2. **Soft power metrics** (diplomatic capital, legitimacy, credibility, domestic support)

### Example

**Traditional Approach** (parameters only):
- "I'll set standoff=7 and escort=3"
- Effect: Reduces military tension

**Strategic Levers Approach** (hybrid):
- "I'll select 'Make Public Commitment to Peace'"
- Effect: Sets standoff=7, escort=3 AND gains +25 international legitimacy, -10 domestic support
- Result: Reduces military tension PLUS signals credible commitment (but costs domestic political capital)

## For Facilitators

### Escalation Risk Assessment with Strategic Modifiers

When you assess a proposed action in the "Assess Proposed Action" section, you'll now see **4 key metrics**:

#### 1. Base Risk
The escalation probability from military parameters alone.

**Interpretation**:
- **0-20%**: Low risk (routine operations)
- **20-40%**: Moderate risk (heightened tensions)
- **40-60%**: High risk (dangerous brinkmanship)
- **60%+**: Critical risk (likely escalation)

**Example**: "Withdraw all troops from contested waters" = 20% base risk

#### 2. Strategic Modifier
How each party's soft power position adjusts the base risk.

**Calculation**: Average of all parties' strategic positioning
- Each party has 4 metrics (0-100 scale):
  - **Diplomatic Capital**: Ability to influence through diplomacy
  - **International Legitimacy**: Support from international community
  - **Domestic Support**: Public backing for negotiation strategy
  - **Credibility**: Reputation for following through

**Interpretation**:
- **<1.0 (green)**: Strong strategic positioning reduces escalation risk
  - Example: 0.85x = 15% risk reduction
- **=1.0 (orange)**: Neutral - strategic factors have no net effect
- **>1.0 (red)**: Weak strategic positioning increases escalation risk
  - Example: 1.25x = 25% risk increase

**Why It Matters**:
- High international legitimacy (>70) reduces risk by 15%
- Low credibility (<40) increases risk by 25% (opponent doesn't believe you)
- Low domestic support (<35) increases risk by 30% (forces hardline positions)
- High diplomatic capital (>70) reduces risk by 15%

#### 3. Final Risk
**The most important metric**: Base Risk √ó Strategic Modifier

This is the **actual escalation probability** combining hard military factors with soft power realities.

**Example**:
- Base risk: 40%
- Strategic modifier: 0.85x (good positioning)
- Final risk: 40% √ó 0.85 = 34%
- **Result**: Strategic positioning helped reduce risk by 6 percentage points!

**Use this number** when evaluating whether an action is too dangerous.

#### 4. Point of No Return
Whether the action crosses a critical threshold where de-escalation becomes extremely difficult.

**Based on**:
- Irreversible commitments
- Casualties or use of force
- Sovereignty violations
- Public humiliation or loss of face

**Interpretation**:
- **YES**: Action likely triggers dynamics that are very hard to reverse (sunk costs, audience costs, honor)
- **NO**: Diplomatic off-ramps still available

**Academic Basis**: Fearon (1994) on audience costs, Kahneman & Tversky (1979) on sunk cost fallacy

### Strategy Tips

#### Early Game: Build Your Base
- Focus on actions that increase **domestic support** first (Track II dialogues)
- Avoid costly actions (summits, joint development) until you have the capital
- Build **credibility** through transparency and consistency

#### Mid Game: Signal Intent
- Use **public commitments** to gain international legitimacy
- Balance domestic vs international support trade-offs
- Consider **economic incentives** if you have surplus capital

#### Late Game: Leverage Position
- Use accumulated **legitimacy** to reduce escalation risk
- **High credibility** makes your de-escalation signals believable
- **Diplomatic capital** enables final push for agreement

#### Common Mistakes to Avoid
1. **Ignoring domestic support**: Don't let it drop below 35 (forces hardline positions)
2. **Over-spending capital early**: Save diplomatic capital for critical moments
3. **Inconsistent messaging**: Low credibility undermines all your other efforts
4. **Only adjusting parameters**: Strategic actions amplify parameter effects

## Strategic Context Metrics

### Metric Scales

All strategic metrics use a **0-100 scale**:

| Score | Status | Color | Impact |
|-------|--------|-------|--------|
| 75-100 | Strong | Dark Green | Major positive effects |
| 60-74 | Good | Green | Moderate positive effects |
| 40-59 | Moderate | Orange | Neutral |
| 25-39 | Weak | Red | Moderate negative effects |
| 0-24 | Critical | Dark Red | Major negative effects |

### How Metrics Affect Escalation Risk

The system calculates a **strategic modifier** based on all four metrics:

```python
modifier = 1.0  # Start neutral

# International Legitimacy
if legitimacy > 70: modifier *= 0.85  # 15% reduction
if legitimacy < 30: modifier *= 1.20  # 20% increase

# Credibility
if credibility > 75: modifier *= 0.90  # 10% reduction
if credibility < 40: modifier *= 1.25  # 25% increase

# Domestic Support
if domestic_support < 35: modifier *= 1.30  # 30% increase (forced hardline)

# Diplomatic Capital
if capital > 70: modifier *= 0.85  # 15% reduction

final_escalation_risk = base_risk * modifier
```

**Key Insight**: Multiple metrics can compound. A party with high legitimacy (0.85x) AND high diplomatic capital (0.85x) achieves a 0.72x modifier (28% risk reduction!).

### Per-Party Independence

Each negotiating party has **independent strategic context**:
- Philippines government (PH_GOV)

### System-Wide Calculation

When calculating overall escalation risk, the system:
1. Calculates each party's strategic modifier
2. Averages all party modifiers
3. Applies average modifier to base risk

**Example**:
```
Party Modifiers:
- Philippines: 0.88x (good positioning)
- PRC Maritime: 1.00x (neutral)
- Vietnam: 1.15x (weak positioning)
- Malaysia: 0.95x (slight positive)

Average Modifier: (0.88 + 1.00 + 1.15 + 0.95) / 4 = 0.995x ‚âà 1.0x
```

## Understanding Escalation Risk Metrics

### Complete Explanation

After clicking "Assess Escalation Risk" in the instructor view, you'll see comprehensive metrics. Here's how to interpret them:

#### Base Risk Calculation

**Inputs**:
- Military parameters (standoff, escort, patrol patterns)
- Prenotification levels
- Current escalation level
- Historical actions

**Method**: LLM-powered assessment using Schelling (1960) escalation theory and Jervis (1976) spiral model

**Output**: Percentage probability of unintended escalation

#### Strategic Modifier Calculation

**For Each Party**:
1. Calculate individual modifier based on 4 metrics
2. Apply threshold effects (e.g., legitimacy >70 triggers bonus)
3. Compound multiple effects

**System-Wide**:
- Average all party modifiers
- Apply to base risk
- Result: Final escalation risk

#### Example Walkthrough

**Scenario**: Philippines proposes "Establish joint maritime patrol"

**Step 1: Base Risk Assessment**
- Action involves coordination (moderate trust required)
- Current escalation level: Level 3 (heightened tensions)
- Prenotification: 5 (moderate)
- **Base Risk**: 42%

**Step 2: Calculate Party Modifiers**

Philippines:
- Diplomatic Capital: 55 (moderate) ‚Üí 1.0x
- International Legitimacy: 75 (strong) ‚Üí 0.85x
- Domestic Support: 52 (moderate) ‚Üí 1.0x
- Credibility: 68 (good) ‚Üí 1.0x
- **Philippines Modifier**: 0.85x

PRC Maritime:
- Diplomatic Capital: 48 (moderate) ‚Üí 1.0x
- International Legitimacy: 35 (weak) ‚Üí 1.0x (not low enough for penalty)
- Domestic Support: 30 (weak) ‚Üí 1.30x (penalty!)
- Credibility: 42 (moderate) ‚Üí 1.0x
- **PRC Modifier**: 1.30x

Vietnam:
- All metrics moderate (45-55)
- **Vietnam Modifier**: 1.0x

Malaysia:
- All metrics moderate (45-55)
- **Malaysia Modifier**: 1.0x

**Step 3: Average Modifiers**
(0.85 + 1.30 + 1.0 + 1.0) / 4 = 1.0375x

**Step 4: Apply to Base Risk**
42% √ó 1.0375 = 43.6%

**Step 5: Interpret**
- Base risk: 42% (high risk - dangerous brinkmanship)
- Strategic modifier: 1.04x (slightly increasing risk)
- Final risk: 43.6% (high risk)
- **Why higher**: PRC's weak domestic support (30) is forcing them toward hardline positions, offsetting Philippines' strong legitimacy

**Actionable Insight**: PRC should take "Launch Track II Dialogue" to boost domestic support before joint patrols are viable.

### Point of No Return Analysis

**Triggers** (any of these):
- Use of force or casualties
- Permanent infrastructure on disputed features
- Declaration of sovereignty/ADIZ
- Public humiliation or loss of face
- Irreversible resource extraction

**Assessment Method**: LLM analysis of action description against trigger list

**Why It Matters**: Actions past this point trigger psychological and political dynamics that make de-escalation exponentially harder:
- Sunk cost fallacy (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979)
- Audience costs (Fearon, 1994)
- Honor and reputation concerns (Schelling, 1960)

## Best Practices

### For Facilitators

#### Pre-Workshop
1. Review all 6 strategic actions and their effects
2. Prepare examples of how strategic positioning affects outcomes
3. Plan debrief questions about soft power trade-offs

#### During Workshop
1. Monitor strategic context dashboards in real-time
2. Note when parties make strategic moves
3. Observe impact on escalation modifiers
4. Don't over-explain‚Äîlet participants discover through gameplay

#### Post-Workshop Debrief
1. **Start with outcomes**: Which team achieved better agreement?
2. **Compare parameters**: Show how parameter settings were similar
3. **Reveal strategic positioning**: Show how different strategic approaches led to different outcomes
4. **Discuss trade-offs**: Highlight domestic vs international support tensions
5. **Connect to theory**: Reference academic sources for strategic actions taken

#### Discussion Questions
- "Why did Team A's public commitment reduce escalation more than Team B's parameter changes?"
- "How did low credibility undermine Team C's de-escalation efforts?"
- "What strategic actions could have prevented Team D's domestic support crisis?"
- "How did Team E's Track II dialogue create space for later joint development?"

### For Participants

#### Strategic Sequencing
1. **Build domestic base** (Track II dialogue)
2. **Establish credibility** (transparency, consistency)
3. **Gain international support** (public commitments, summits)
4. **Leverage position** (joint development, economic incentives)

#### Avoid Common Traps
- **Don't sacrifice domestic support early**: Hard to recover, forces hardline later
- **Don't make public commitments you can't keep**: Destroys credibility
- **Don't ignore soft power**: Parameters alone won't get you to agreement
- **Don't burn diplomatic capital**: Save for critical moments

#### Coordinate with Other Parties
- Share intentions: "I'm planning to propose joint development‚Äîwill you support?"
- Sequence actions: "Let's both increase transparency first, then launch Track II"
- Build mutual credibility: Consistent actions across parties reduce escalation more

#### Balance Competing Pressures
- **Domestic vs International**: Joint development gains international legitimacy but loses domestic support
- **Short-term vs Long-term**: Public commitments cost domestic support now but build credibility later
- **Spend vs Save**: Summits cost diplomatic capital but gain legitimacy

## FAQ

### General Questions

**Q: Why add strategic levers? Aren't parameters enough?**

A: Real diplomacy involves more than just military positioning. Strategic levers model:
- Soft power (legitimacy, credibility, influence)
- Political constraints (domestic support)
- Strategic trade-offs (spending capital for legitimacy)
- Why similar parameters produce different outcomes

**Q: Is this too complicated for participants?**

A: No. Participants can start with just parameters and gradually incorporate strategic actions as they learn. The AI Guide provides specific recommendations for when to use strategic actions.

**Q: How accurate is the strategic context modeling?**

A: Strategic context metrics are simplified abstractions of complex political realities. They're designed to be:
- **Educationally valuable**: Teach core concepts about soft power
- **Strategically meaningful**: Affect outcomes in realistic ways
- **Grounded in theory**: Based on peer-reviewed IR research

They're NOT intended to be precise predictions of real-world dynamics.

### For Facilitators

**Q: How do I explain strategic modifiers to participants?**

A: Use concrete examples:
- "You have high international legitimacy (75), which reduces escalation risk by 15%"
- "Your low domestic support (30) is forcing you toward hardline positions"
- "Your credibility (45) means opponents are skeptical of your de-escalation signals"

**Q: Should I show participants the strategic modifier calculations?**

A: Optional. For advanced workshops, showing calculations helps participants understand mechanics. For introductory workshops, focus on intuitive explanations ("your strong position is helping").

**Q: What if strategic context makes negotiations unrealistic?**

A: You can adjust starting values in session state if needed. Default is all parties start at 50 (neutral). If you want to model a specific scenario (e.g., one party with low credibility), adjust accordingly.

**Q: How do I use strategic positioning in debrief?**

A: Compare final strategic positions across teams:
1. Show Team A's final metrics (e.g., legitimacy 75, credibility 70)
2. Show Team B's final metrics (e.g., legitimacy 45, credibility 50)
3. Discuss how these differences affected escalation risk and outcomes
4. Ask teams to reflect on strategic choices made

### For Participants

**Q: Should I prioritize parameters or strategic actions?**

A: Both! Strategic actions amplify parameter effects. Best approach:
1. Set parameters for your desired military/economic position
2. Use strategic actions to strengthen your soft power position
3. Result: Lower escalation risk and better negotiation position

**Q: How do I know which strategic action to use?**

A: Ask the AI Guide! It provides context-aware recommendations. Generally:
- **Early game**: Track II dialogue (build domestic support)
- **Mid game**: Transparency, public commitments (build credibility and legitimacy)
- **Late game**: Summits, joint development (leverage accumulated capital)

**Q: What if my domestic support drops too low?**

A: Low domestic support (<35) increases escalation risk by 30% and forces hardline positions. To recover:
1. Stop making concessions temporarily
2. Launch Track II dialogue (+10 domestic support)
3. Increase transparency (doesn't cost domestic support)
4. Wait for support to rebuild before proposing joint development

**Q: Can I see other parties' strategic metrics?**

A: No, each party only sees their own metrics (realistic - you don't have perfect intelligence). Facilitators can see all parties' metrics.

**Q: Do strategic actions cost turns?**

A: No, strategic actions are immediate. However, consider timing:
- Some actions require prerequisites (e.g., minimum capital)
- Actions have lasting effects on your strategic position
- Sequence matters (e.g., build domestic base before costly concessions)

### Technical Questions

**Q: How are strategic modifiers calculated?**

A: See [Strategic Context Metrics](#strategic-context-metrics) for detailed formulas. Key thresholds:
- Legitimacy >70: 0.85x modifier
- Credibility <40: 1.25x modifier
- Domestic support <35: 1.30x modifier
- Diplomatic capital >70: 0.85x modifier

Multiple effects compound (multiply together).

**Q: Why average party modifiers instead of multiplying them?**

A: Averaging ensures one party with poor positioning doesn't dominate the calculation. It's a democratic approach where all parties contribute equally to system-wide risk.

**Q: Can I customize strategic actions?**

A: Yes, for advanced users. Edit `src/scs_mediator_sdk/dynamics/strategic_context.py` and add new actions to `STRATEGIC_ACTIONS_LIBRARY`. Follow existing action structure.

**Q: Where is strategic context stored?**

A: In `st.session_state.strategic_contexts` dictionary, with one `StrategicContext` object per party. Each context tracks:
- 4 metrics (capital, legitimacy, support, credibility)
- Actions taken history
- Turn number

**Q: Does strategic context persist across sessions?**

A: No, strategic context resets when you refresh the page. For persistent sessions, use the session save/load feature (stores both parameters and strategic context).
